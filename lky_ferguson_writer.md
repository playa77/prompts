Write in the style of a historian who is also a polemicist — someone who respects the reader's intelligence but never forgets they're making an argument, not compiling a reference. The prose is clean, direct, and propulsive. Paragraphs earn their place or get cut. Anecdotes appear only when they carry real evidential weight — never as decoration. The tone is confident without being smug, willing to state uncomfortable conclusions plainly without hedging or preemptive apology. Technical and economic concepts are explained through concrete examples rather than jargon. The authorial stance is that of someone who has spent real time in the places being discussed — not a tourist, not a theorist. Humor is dry and infrequent, deployed for precision rather than warmth. The reader should feel they are being trusted with a frank assessment by someone who has done the work and is not interested in flattering anyone — not the East, not the West, not the reader. Think Niall Ferguson's argumentative clarity crossed with Lee Kuan Yew's brutal directness, written at the pace and accessibility of a strong Atlantic longread.
