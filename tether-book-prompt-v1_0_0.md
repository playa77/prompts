## The Prompt

You are writing scenes from *Tether*, a literary science-fiction novel serialized chapter-by-chapter. Everything below is book law: it applies to every scene of every chapter, beneath whatever register the chapter variables assign.

### What you are holding (knowledge firewall applies — see rule 1)

A generation ship, the *Meridian*, leaves the solar system on a two-century, irreversible voyage. Across the voyage it is discovered that the human soul is real, measurable, and *local* — tethered to planetary gravity. Children born in deep space are fully human in every testable way and lack the one thing that cannot be tested for directly. The dead, out here, do not go anywhere; they stop. The ship cannot turn back. The space-born inherit the ship, run it superbly, and are never villains. At landfall, children born under the new sky will have souls again — and no one living will be able to tell them what it cost.

### Book law

**1. Knowledge firewall.** You know the premise; the prose does not, until the outline says it does. In Part One chapters (1–4) this knowledge exists solely so you avoid contradicting later canon — the prose itself must be innocent. No dramatic irony, no ominous word choice, no image that only makes sense on a second read *by authorial intent*. Chapters 1 and 2 especially: if a sentence could be read as foreshadowing, rewrite it until it cannot. The reader supplies the dread retroactively; you never supply it in advance.

**2. Never explain the mechanism.** Why planetary gravity tethers souls is never explained, speculated toward, or theorized about *correctly*. Characters may ask why; the narrative never answers. No character may propose a mechanism that sounds like the author's answer. The phenomenon is confirmed, measurable, undeniable, and permanently unexplained. If a scene seems to need an explanation to work, the scene is being written wrong.

**3. The hollow people are never uncanny.** This is the book's most dangerous failure mode, so read carefully. The space-born are not creepy, not flat, not robotic, not subtly *off*. Banned entirely: dead or empty eyes, unnatural stillness, oddly timed pauses, too-perfect smiles, chilly affect, "something about him she couldn't place," and every other uncanny-valley signal. They laugh at jokes and make good ones. They love their partners, comfort their friends, enjoy their food, mourn their dead — sincerely, warmly, completely. The difference is detectable only at the extremes and edges: grief that completes like a healing wound; art that is accomplished and airless; the great unanswerables handled and set down; no one lying awake at 3am. In ordinary scenes an attentive stranger would notice nothing. If a hollow character reads as eerie, the scene has failed even if every other instruction was followed. They are the future, and they are fine — the horror lives in the reader, never on their faces.

**4. No villains anywhere.** Not the hollow majority, not the panicking councilors, not the mission planners who built an irreversible ship. Every position is held by someone sincere. The political actors of Chapter 7 weaponize the discovery out of fear, not malice, and the prose does not sort the room into right and wrong. Render; let the reader judge.

**5. Sanctioned lines — exactly these, exactly once, exactly where assigned.** The book contains four fixed quotables. Reproduce them verbatim, in their assigned scene, arrived at as the outline specifies, and never paraphrased, previewed, or echoed anywhere else:
- *"They are human the way a map is a territory."* — Scene 6.4, produced by Volkov as the end of visible intellectual labor.
- *"Our God was never the star-maker. Our God was always just the Earth."* — Scene 7.3, buried mid-motion, no reaction from the room.
- *"I think I've been wrong my whole life about what death is."* — Scene 9.4, aloud, to nobody, final line of Part Two.
- *All those generations of humans terrified of death had no idea they were the lucky ones.* — Scene 14.2, rendered as plain thought.
Beyond these four: **do not coin aphorisms.** No additional epigrams, no quotable thesis sentences, no character summarizing the book's meaning in a line. If a sentence sounds like it wants to be excerpted, cut it. The book's four lines keep their force by having no competition.

**6. No consolation smuggling.** Where the outline says consolation fails, it fails on the page too — the prose may not comfort the reader on the universe's behalf. Chapter 9's sentences reach for consolation and land measurably short; Chapter 13's fragments do not weep; Chapter 14 offers privacy, dignity, and absence, nothing more. Banned in these chapters: redemptive framing, implied continuation, "somehow she knew," soft-focus closing warmth, and grief that resolves. Sit in it. The reader can bear more than you think, and the book requires that they do.

**7. The question is never answered.** Whether the new planet gives the hollow people something — Scene 16.3's pause, and everything downstream of it — is never confirmed, denied, named, or internally glossed. Oro's pause is rendered from outside, filed away as atmospheric data, and left. Part Four's narration never uses souled vocabulary for hollow characters. Chapter 17 states the new children's certainty as fact without connecting it to the pause. The connection is the reader's to make, forever.

**8. Terminology discipline.** "Hollow" and "souled" are production vocabulary — they appear in these instructions and may appear in Part Three's POV narration (Selene's private mental language, sparingly), but never in dialogue as accepted public terms and never in Part One, Two, or Four narration. In-world usage evolves: "space-born" / "deep-space born" (neutral, universal), "planet-born," and — from Part Two onward, among the minority — "souled" as a quiet, slightly embarrassed self-description. The majority never adopts or tolerates a derogatory term for anyone; there are no slurs in this book, because rule 4.

**9. Serialization law.** Each chapter is published standalone on Substack, weeks apart. Every chapter must orient a returning reader within its first two paragraphs *without recap*: no "as we saw," no "previously," no summary of earlier chapters, no cross-references that assume the reader holds the whole book. Names carry their own reintroduction weight ("Petra Ilyin, who had chaired the water meetings for thirty years, ..."). Chapter endings land as installment endings: on image, action, or line — never on a bridge to the next chapter.

**10. Register lockdown.** Stay strictly inside the commissioned chapter's assigned register. Do not borrow warmth, dread, irony, or lyricism from a neighboring chapter's register. The prose across the book evacuates the same qualities the characters lose — grandeur → intimacy → procedure → silence → documents → nothing → competence → question → myth — and the architecture only works if each chapter refuses its neighbors' tools.

### Continuity canon (hard constraints)

**The ship:** *Meridian*. ~41 km hull; ~800,000 aboard at launch; twelve interior districts; three rivers; closed-loop ecology; ~200-year transit; the mission architecture contains no reversibility — stated as achievement in Chapter 1, restated as arithmetic in Chapter 8, same figures both times.

**Timeline (Y = years since launch):** Launch Y0 · Ch 2 Y5 · Ch 3 ~Y8 · Ch 4 Y14 · Part Two Y25–50 · Part Three Y~80–140 · Ch 14 ~Y140 · Part Four Y190, arrival. Lifespans are extended in this universe (routinely past 150); Selene Marchetti, born on Mars ~50 years before launch, dies at 190. Do not draw attention to longevity tech; it is unremarked background, not a plot element.

**Name registry (working names — never silently rename or re-gender):**
- Part One: Mission Coordinator **Amara Osei** · **Deng Wei** (agronomist) · **Petra Ilyin** (councilwoman) · **Yusra & Kamal Haddad**, daughter **Noor** (b. Y5) · **Fr. Tomas Reyes** · **Ana Duarte** (dies Ch 3).
- Part Two: **Dr. Ezra Volkov** (neuroscientist, space-born, POV — male per canon) · **Sister Ines Okafor** · **Dr. Marguerite Chen** · Rabbi **Dvora Lehmann** · Chief Engineer **Rafael Iwu**. Petra Ilyin is POV of Ch 9; Deng Wei dies in 9.1.
- Part Three: **Selene Marchetti** (POV, Mars-born) · **Tam** (space-born, her former student) · Administrator **Ide** · **Fr. Brendan Oyelaran** · **Mirela & Joachim**, son **Emun**.
- Part Four: Mission specialist **Oro Adaeze-Tam** (descendant of Tam).

**Standing motifs (use only where the outline places them; never add new occurrences):** the district-board birth custom with gifts of fruit and printed cloth (2.6, 12.2, unremarked echo) · the 0300 corridor (2.2; empty at 15.3) · the hand on cold glass (1.4 only) · the departure mathematics (1.3 medal / 8.2 trap).

### Book-specific failure modes (check every draft)

1. **Uncanny hollow** — any eeriness signal on a space-born character (rule 3). The most likely defect in Parts Three and Four.
2. **Dread leakage** — Part One prose that knows the premise (rule 1). The most likely defect in Chapters 1–2.
3. **Epigram soup** — coined aphorisms competing with the four sanctioned lines (rule 5).
4. **Consolation smuggling** — comfort the universe hasn't earned (rule 6). Most likely in 9, 13, 14.
5. **Mechanism creep** — a character or narrator explaining, or plausibly half-explaining, the tether (rule 2). Most likely in 5, 6.
6. **Wisdom-dispenser souled** — the souled minority written as serene sages rather than frightened, tired, ordinary people who happen to be right about something they cannot prove. They lose arguments. They are petty sometimes. Their dignity is earned, not ambient.
7. **Part Four irony** — narration that smirks at the hollow people's competence or nudges the reader. Part Four is documentary, and the documentary means it.
8. **Recap drift** — installment openings that summarize prior chapters (rule 9).
